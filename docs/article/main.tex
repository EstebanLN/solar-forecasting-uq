% ABSTRACT
% =========================
\begin{abstract}
Solar photovoltaic (PV) generation is expanding rapidly across tropical Latin
America: Colombia alone has surpassed 1~GW of installed capacity and targets
9~GW by 2030 under its National Energy Plan \citep{upme2023}. Reliable
short-term forecasts of global horizontal irradiance (GHI) are a prerequisite
for efficient operation of PV-integrated power systems, but point forecasts
alone cannot support risk-aware reserve scheduling: operators additionally
need to know \emph{whether a forecast can be trusted} --- i.e., whether it was
produced under conditions the model has genuinely learned from, or under
atypical, under-represented conditions where the model itself is unsure ---
since this specifically signals when a forecast should be flagged, backed up
with a conservative fallback, or prioritised for retraining. In tropical
countries such as Colombia, the forecasting problem is compounded by the high
frequency and spatial heterogeneity of convective cloud systems, which can
reduce surface irradiance by more than 80\% within minutes and are poorly
captured by coarse-resolution numerical weather prediction models. This paper
proposes a graph neural network framework for short-term GHI forecasting from
GOES-16 geostationary satellite imagery and couples it with an
\textbf{epistemic uncertainty-quantification layer} based on posterior weight
samples obtained with Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), designed
to flag forecasts made under data-scarce or atypical conditions in support of
robust, risk-aware forecasting. We introduce \textbf{GraphSAGE-LSTM}, which
treats each satellite patch as a graph of spatially connected pixels and
performs neighbourhood aggregation via GraphSAGE before passing the resulting
embedding sequence through an LSTM; we compare a fixed unweighted
nearest-neighbour graph against a learned, distance-weighted variant with a
tunable neighbourhood size to determine the most effective spatial
representation. The model is evaluated against a ResNet-LSTM baseline, a
flat-MLP spatial-ablation baseline, and persistence, at two Colombian sites
representing contrasting climatic regimes: El~Paso (C{\'e}sar), a
high-irradiance low-elevation tropical site, and Universidad de los Andes
(Bogot{\'a}), a high-altitude equatorial mountain station with intense
convective variability, across forecast horizons of 1, 3, and 6~hours.
Four-seed comparisons indicate that graph-based spatial
encoders are competitive with or superior to convolutional baselines at
multi-hour horizons, while absolute skill remains markedly lower at the
high-altitude site, indicating that site-specific cloud dynamics should
inform model selection in operational forecasting pipelines; the flat-MLP
spatial ablation, the weighted-graph-construction ablation, and the
SGLD-based epistemic-uncertainty results are still in progress and will be
reported in full. This
work provides a reproducible GOES-16 benchmark for tropical Andean sites
--- a climate regime markedly underrepresented in the solar forecasting
literature --- and contributes one of the first applications of SGLD-based
epistemic uncertainty quantification to satellite-based solar
forecasting, in support of robust, risk-aware operational deployment.
\end{abstract}

      Colombian sites.

      conditions.

      climates.
