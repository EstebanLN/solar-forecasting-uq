% ABSTRACT
% =========================
\begin{abstract}
Solar photovoltaic (PV) generation is expanding rapidly across tropical Latin
America: Colombia alone has surpassed 1~GW of installed capacity and targets
9~GW by 2030 under its National Energy Plan \citep{upme2023}. Reliable
short-term forecasts of global horizontal irradiance (GHI) are essential for
risk-aware reserve scheduling in PV-integrated power systems, yet a point
forecast alone cannot signal whether it was produced under conditions the
model has genuinely learned from or under atypical, data-scarce conditions
--- a distinction that determines whether the forecast should be trusted or
flagged for a conservative fallback. In tropical countries such as Colombia,
forecasting is further complicated by frequent, heterogeneous convective
cloud systems that can reduce surface irradiance by more than 80\% within
minutes and are poorly captured by coarse-resolution numerical weather
prediction. This paper proposes a graph neural network
framework for short-term GHI forecasting from GOES-16 geostationary imagery,
coupled with an \textbf{epistemic uncertainty-quantification layer} based on
Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) posterior weight sampling that
flags forecasts made under data-scarce or atypical conditions. We introduce
\textbf{GraphSAGE-LSTM}, which treats each satellite patch as a graph of
spatially connected pixels, aggregates neighbourhoods via GraphSAGE, and
passes the resulting embedding sequence through an LSTM, comparing a fixed
unweighted nearest-neighbour graph against a tuned, distance-weighted
variant with a tunable neighbourhood size. The model is evaluated against
ResNet-LSTM, FlatMLP, and persistence baselines at two climatically
contrasting Colombian sites --- El~Paso (C{\'e}sar), a high-irradiance
lowland site, and Universidad de los Andes (Bogot{\'a}), a high-altitude
equatorial mountain station --- across 1-, 3-, and 6-hour horizons. Four-seed
comparisons show graph-based encoders are competitive with or superior to
convolutional baselines at multi-hour horizons, while absolute skill is
markedly lower at the mountain site, indicating that model selection should
be climate-aware; the FlatMLP, weighted-graph, and SGLD results are in
progress. This work contributes a
reproducible GOES-16 benchmark for the underrepresented tropical Andean
climate and one of the first applications of SGLD-based epistemic
uncertainty quantification to satellite-based solar forecasting.
\end{abstract}
