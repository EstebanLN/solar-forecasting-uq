% ABSTRACT
% =========================
\begin{abstract}
Solar photovoltaic (PV) generation is expanding rapidly across tropical Latin
America: Colombia alone has surpassed 1~GW of installed capacity and targets
9~GW by 2030 under its National Energy Plan \cite{upme2023}. Reliable
short-term forecasts of global horizontal irradiance (GHI) are a prerequisite
for efficient operation of PV-integrated power systems, supporting reserve
scheduling, reducing imbalance penalties, and enabling real-time dispatch
decisions. In tropical countries such as Colombia, the forecasting problem is
compounded by the high frequency and spatial heterogeneity of convective cloud
systems, which can reduce surface irradiance by more than 80\% within minutes
and are poorly captured by coarse-resolution numerical weather prediction
models. This paper proposes a graph neural network framework for short-term
GHI forecasting from GOES-16 geostationary satellite imagery and couples it
with conformal uncertainty quantification (UQ) to produce calibrated
prediction intervals suitable for risk-aware operational decisions. We
introduce \textbf{GraphSAGE-LSTM}, which treats each satellite patch as a
graph of spatially connected pixels, performs neighbourhood aggregation via
GraphSAGE with inverse-distance edge weights, and passes the resulting
embedding sequence through an LSTM. The model is evaluated against a
ResNet-LSTM baseline, a flat-MLP spatial-ablation baseline, and persistence,
at two Colombian sites representing contrasting climatic regimes: El~Paso
(C{\'e}sar), a high-irradiance low-elevation tropical site, and Universidad de
los Andes (Bogot{\'a}), a high-altitude equatorial mountain station with
intense convective variability. Across forecast horizons of 1, 3, and 6~hours
and four independent random seeds, GraphSAGE-LSTM achieves the best skill
among the deep-learning models at the 6-hour horizon at both sites
($\skillday = 0.636 \pm 0.013$ vs.\ $0.614 \pm 0.023$ for ResNet-LSTM at
El~Paso; $0.417 \pm 0.008$ vs.\ $0.368 \pm 0.019$ at Uniandes), with
consistently lower seed-to-seed variance --- while the markedly lower absolute
skill at the high-altitude site indicates that site-specific cloud dynamics
should inform model selection in operational forecasting pipelines.
Split Conformal Prediction is applied as a
post-hoc UQ layer, providing finite-sample marginal coverage guarantees
without distributional assumptions. This work establishes a reproducible
GOES-16 benchmark for tropical Andean sites --- a climate regime markedly
underrepresented in the solar forecasting literature --- and lays the
foundation for a staged UQ roadmap toward adaptive-width intervals and
aleatoric/epistemic uncertainty decomposition.
\end{abstract}
