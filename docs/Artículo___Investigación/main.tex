% ABSTRACT
% =========================
\begin{abstract}
Accurate short-term forecasting of global horizontal irradiance (GHI) is critical
for integrating solar photovoltaic generation into power grids, particularly in
tropical regions where cloud-induced variability is high and numerical weather
prediction (NWP) data are scarce. This paper presents a systematic comparison of
deep-learning architectures for satellite-based GHI forecasting at two Colombian
sites with contrasting climatic regimes: El~Paso (C{\'e}sar), a low-elevation
tropical site, and Universidad de los Andes (Bogot{\'a}), a high-altitude equatorial
mountain station. We propose a GraphSAGE-LSTM model that treats each GOES-16
satellite patch as a graph of spatially connected pixels, aggregating neighbourhood
context before temporal modelling with an LSTM. We compare this against a
ResNet-LSTM baseline, a statistical SARIMAX model on the clear-sky index, and a
persistence reference, across forecast horizons of 1, 3, and 6~hours and four
independent random seeds. GraphSAGE-LSTM achieves a daytime skill score of
$\skillday = 0.636 \pm 0.011$ at the 6-hour horizon for El~Paso, outperforming
the ResNet-LSTM ($0.614 \pm 0.020$) and SARIMAX ($0.210$) baselines. For
Universidad de los Andes, where convective variability reduces the advantage of
spatial deep learning, SARIMAX remains competitive at 6~hours ($0.435$) relative
to GraphSAGE-LSTM ($0.417 \pm 0.007$). At the 1-hour horizon, all deep-learning
models exhibit negative or near-zero skill at El~Paso, underscoring the difficulty
of outperforming persistence at short horizons under clear-sky conditions. These
results demonstrate that graph-based spatial encoding provides a consistent
advantage over convolutional encoding for longer forecast horizons and high-irradiance
regimes, while statistical methods remain a competitive baseline in highly variable
tropical mountain climates.
\end{abstract}
