\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
\subsection{Study sites}

Experiments are conducted at two meteorological stations in Colombia that represent
climatologically contrasting regimes (Table~\ref{tab:sites}).

\textbf{El Paso} is located in the lowland Caribbean region of C{\'e}sar department,
characterised by high annual irradiance, predominantly clear-sky conditions, and
dry-season cloud cover driven by the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ).

\textbf{Universidad de los Andes} (referred to as Uniandes) is an instrumented
rooftop station in the Colombian Andean city of Bogot{\'a}. At 2\,600~m above sea
level, it experiences the equatorial bimodal rainfall regime with intense convective
cloud formation typically peaking in the early afternoon, producing sharp intraday
GHI drops.

\subsection{Data}

\subsubsection{Ground measurements}

GHI is measured at 10-minute resolution and stored in time-zone-aware (UTC) Parquet
files. Missing values account for less than 2\% of the record at both sites and are
excluded during dataset construction.

\subsubsection{Satellite data}

We use the GOES-16 ABI Level-2 Multi-Band Cloud and Moisture Imagery Product
(ABI-L2-MCMIPF), which provides all 16 ABI spectral channels at a unified
10-minute cadence and ${\sim}2$~km spatial resolution in the visible bands.
For each observation timestamp, a $16 \times 16$ pixel patch ($P = 16$) centred
on the target site is extracted, yielding a tensor of shape $(C, P, P) = (16, 16, 16)$
in units consistent with each band's native reflectance or brightness temperature.

\subsubsection{Dataset construction and temporal split}

Forecast samples are constructed as pairs $(X, y)$ where the input
$X \in \R^{L \times C \times P \times P}$ is a sequence of $L = 24$ satellite
patch frames covering the most recent 4 hours of observation, and the target
$y \in \R$ is the GHI at the site $H$ steps ahead. We consider
$H \in \{6, 18, 36\}$ steps (corresponding to 1, 3, and 6 hours at 10-minute
resolution).

To prevent data leakage, train, validation, and test sets are defined by
non-overlapping calendar blocks (Table~\ref{tab:splits}).

\subsection{Models}

\subsubsection{Persistence baseline}

The persistence model assumes irradiance is unchanged over the forecast horizon:
$\hat{y}(t+H) = \text{GHI}(t)$. It is optimal under no-change dynamics and
serves as the standard benchmark for short-term solar forecasting.

\subsubsection{SARIMAX on the clear-sky index}
\label{ssec:sarima}

Raw GHI is non-stationary due to the deterministic diurnal envelope. We first
compute the clear-sky index $k_t$:
\begin{equation}
  k_t = \frac{\text{GHI}_t}{\ghics_t}, \quad
  k_t = 0 \text{ if } \ghics_t < 10\,\text{W/m}^2
  \label{eq:csi}
\end{equation}
where $\ghics$ is the Ineichen clear-sky model \cite{ineichen2002} evaluated with
\texttt{pvlib} \cite{holmgren2018}. The series $k_t$ lies in $[0, 1.5]$ and is
more stationary than raw GHI.

Because the 10-minute resolution would require an intractable seasonal period of
$m = 144$, we resample $k_t$ to hourly resolution ($m = 24$). Order selection
via ADF/KPSS tests, ACF/PACF analysis, and AIC grid search \cite{hyndman2018}
yields the model:
\begin{equation}
  \text{SARIMAX}(2,1,2)(1,1,1)_{24}
  \label{eq:sarima}
\end{equation}

The fitted model forecasts $k_t$ out-of-sample; the final GHI prediction is
recovered as $\hat{\text{GHI}}(t) = \hat{k}(t)\cdot\ghics(t)$.

\subsubsection{ResNet-LSTM}
\label{ssec:resnet}

The ResNet-LSTM encoder-decoder processes the patch sequence $X$ as follows.
A lightweight ResNet \cite{he2016} (three residual stages with stride-2 downsampling,
followed by global average pooling) encodes each frame $(C, P, P)$ into a
$d_e$-dimensional embedding $\mathbf{e}_\ell \in \R^{d_e}$. The sequence
$(\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_L)$ is then processed by a single- or two-layer
LSTM \cite{hochreiter1997} whose final hidden state is projected to the scalar
output $\hat{y}$.

The ResNet encoder has three hyperparameters: base channel width
(\texttt{base}~$\in\{16, 24, 32, 48\}$), embedding dimension
(\texttt{emb\_dim}~$\in\{64, 128, 192\}$), and head dropout. The total parameter
count ranges from approximately 50k to 500k depending on the configuration.

\subsubsection{GraphSAGE-LSTM}
\label{ssec:gsage}

Instead of convolutional layers, we model the spatial patch as a graph
$G = (V, E)$ where each pixel is a node ($|V| = P^2 = 256$) and edges connect
8-connected neighbours (horizontal, vertical, diagonal). Node features at step $\ell$
are the $C = 16$ channel values at that pixel.

GraphSAGE \cite{hamilton2017} updates each node's representation by aggregating
its neighbourhood:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_v^{(k)} = \sigma\!\left(
    \mathbf{W}^{(k)} \left[ \mathbf{h}_v^{(k-1)} \Vert
    \text{MEAN}_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \mathbf{h}_u^{(k-1)} \right]
  \right)
  \label{eq:sage}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}(v)$ denotes the neighbours of $v$, $\Vert$ is concatenation,
and $\sigma$ is ReLU. After $K$ layers (K~$\in\{2,3,4\}$), a mean readout over
all nodes yields the graph-level embedding $\mathbf{g}_\ell \in \R^{d_g}$.
The sequence $(\mathbf{g}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{g}_L)$ is then processed by an
LSTM identical to that of the ResNet-LSTM variant.

The edge index is a fixed buffer registered with the model, so both standard
mini-batch DataLoaders and the existing training loop are reused without
modification.

\subsubsection{Flat MLP baseline (under evaluation)}
\label{ssec:mlp}

As an ablation of spatial structure, we include a \textbf{FlatMLP} baseline that
applies global average pooling over the $P \times P$ spatial dimensions of each
frame, yielding a flattened feature vector of size $L \times C = 24 \times 16 = 384$.
This vector is passed through a multi-layer perceptron with \texttt{LayerNorm} and
\texttt{ReLU} activations. The FlatMLP uses no spatial inductive bias and serves
to quantify the benefit of spatial encoding. Results for this model are pending
and will be included in the final version.

The Optuna study identifies the best trial on validation $\rmseday$; the winning
configuration is then retrained for up to 30~epochs with patience~8 and evaluated
on the held-out test set.

\subsection{Training protocol}

All deep-learning models are trained with the Adam optimiser minimising the MSE
loss on normalised targets ($y_\text{norm} = (y - \bar{y}) / s_y$, with $\bar{y}$
and $s_y$ estimated from the training set). Training uses early stopping with
patience~6 (trials) or~8 (final retraining). Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) is
enabled when a GPU is available. Each experiment is replicated with four seeds
$\{42, 1, 7, 13\}$ to report mean~$\pm$~std statistics.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics}

Following standard practice in solar forecasting \cite{coimbra2013}, we report:

\textbf{Daytime RMSE} ($\rmseday$, W/m²): RMSE computed only over test samples
with observed $\text{GHI} \geq 20$~W/m², excluding night-time samples where all
models trivially predict zero.

\textbf{Daytime skill score} ($\skillday$): normalised improvement over persistence,
\begin{equation}
  \skillday = 1 - \frac{\rmseday(\text{model})}{\rmseday(\text{persistence})}
  \label{eq:skill}
\end{equation}
A score of 0 indicates parity with persistence; positive values indicate improvement.
We also report $\text{MAE}_\text{day}$ for completeness.
