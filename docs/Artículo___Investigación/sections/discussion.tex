\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Why does GraphSAGE outperform ResNet at longer horizons?}

At the 6-hour horizon for El~Paso, GraphSAGE-LSTM achieves a 3.6-percentage-point
skill improvement over ResNet-LSTM ($0.636$ vs.\ $0.614$). We hypothesise two
mechanisms:

\textbf{Irregular spatial structure.} At longer lead times, the relevant cloud
signal migrates from the centre of the patch (where it directly causes GHI
reduction) toward the periphery, where incoming cloud advection is reflected in
the satellite channels. CNN filters, being translation-equivariant, apply the same
spatial template across all positions; GraphSAGE, by contrast, learns separate
aggregation rules that can weight distant pixels differently depending on their
graph neighbourhood. This flexibility may be especially valuable for detecting
cloud-edge gradients that precede GHI events.

\textbf{Reduced redundancy.} The ResNet applies multiple stacked convolutions
that effectively redundantly process the spatial information across layers; the
GraphSAGE message-passing is applied once per hop and is less prone to
over-smoothing at the patch scale ($P = 16$). The lower seed variance of
GraphSAGE ($\pm 0.011$ vs.\ $\pm 0.020$ at El~Paso 6h) is consistent with a
more stable loss landscape.

\subsection{Why does SARIMAX remain competitive at Uniandes 6h?}

The finding that SARIMAX ($\skillday = 0.435$) outperforms GraphSAGE-LSTM ($0.417$)
at the 6-hour horizon for Uniandes is counterintuitive given the richer information
available to the DL models. We attribute it to the \emph{predictability structure}
of the Bogot{\'a} climate:

\textbf{Diurnal regularity.} At $4.6°\text{N}$, the solar declination varies little
across the year ($\pm 4.6°$), producing a highly repeatable clear-sky envelope.
Cloud formation in Bogot{\'a} is predominantly triggered by orographic lifting in
the early afternoon, creating a systematic diurnal pattern that the seasonal MA
and AR terms of SARIMAX($\cdot$)(1,1,1)$_{24}$ can capture efficiently.

\textbf{Satellite signal saturation.} At 6 hours, the satellite patch at $t$
may have low predictive relevance for the ground GHI at $t+6\text{h}$: the cloud
system that will arrive has not yet entered the patch. SARIMAX effectively uses
the historical autocorrelation of $k_t$ (which encodes the regular afternoon cloud
cycle) as its predictive feature, a signal that does not degrade with forecast
horizon in the same way that satellite spatial features do.

This result suggests that \emph{site-specific climatological structure should inform
model selection}: in climates with strong, regular diurnal cloud patterns,
statistical baselines may remain competitive at multi-hour horizons even when
high-resolution satellite data are available.

\subsection{Why is the 1-hour horizon intractable at El~Paso?}

The negative skill at 1-hour El~Paso (all DL models, $\skillday \approx -0.17$
to $-0.20$) can be explained by the \emph{low persistence error}: at 204.4~W/m²,
persistence is already very accurate because the predominantly clear-sky regime
means irradiance rarely drops by more than a small fraction over 10 minutes.
The DL models, trained to minimise MSE over the full distribution (including
the occasional cloud event), learn a slightly smoothed version of the target
trajectory that incurs higher error on the flat clear-sky majority.

A potential remedy is a loss function that differentially weights cloud events
(e.g., weighted MSE by clear-sky departure $|k_t - 1|$), or dedicated cloud-event
detection as a preprocessing step.

\subsection{Practical implications}

\begin{itemize}
  \item For \textbf{El~Paso-type sites} (high irradiance, moderate variability),
        GraphSAGE-LSTM with Optuna-tuned hyperparameters is the recommended model
        at 3h and 6h horizons, with a 60+\% reduction in RMSE over persistence.
        At 1h, the system should fall back to persistence or a light-weight
        nowcasting model.

  \item For \textbf{Uniandes-type sites} (tropical mountain, high convective
        variability), SARIMAX on the clear-sky index provides a surprisingly strong
        baseline at 6h and should be included in any benchmarking suite. DL models
        add modest value but require careful hyperparameter tuning to outperform it.

  \item The high seed variance of ResNet-LSTM at El~Paso 3h ($\pm 0.090$) suggests
        that multiple retraining runs should be averaged for production deployment,
        or that GraphSAGE-LSTM should be preferred for its higher stability.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations and future work}

Several limitations of the current study should be acknowledged:

\textbf{Single-site evaluation.} Although two sites are used, both are located in
Colombia and represent a subset of tropical climate sub-types. Generalisation to
other tropical or subtropical regions requires additional validation.

\textbf{Flat MLP baseline pending.} The FlatMLP ablation (Section~\ref{ssec:mlp}),
which quantifies the benefit of any spatial encoding relative to simple
spatially-pooled features, is currently under evaluation and will be incorporated
in the final analysis.

\textbf{Uncertainty quantification.} All models currently produce point forecasts.
Grid operators require probabilistic outputs: prediction intervals that are
well-calibrated across conditions of varying cloud cover. We have implemented
Split Conformal Prediction \cite{angelopoulos2023} as a post-hoc calibration
layer and are evaluating its coverage properties. Future work will also include
Gaussian variance head training (NLL loss) and Stochastic Gradient Langevin
Dynamics (SGLD) \cite{welling2011} for approximate Bayesian posterior sampling,
which provides epistemic uncertainty decomposition.

\textbf{Multi-step output.} Current models produce a single-step forecast at
horizon $H$. Simultaneous multi-step output (e.g., producing forecasts for all
three horizons from a single forward pass) could improve consistency across
horizon profiles and reduce inference cost.
